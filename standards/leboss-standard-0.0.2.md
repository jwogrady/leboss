## Normative Keywords

The key words **MUST**, **MUST NOT**, **SHOULD**, **SHOULD NOT**, and **MAY** in this document are to be interpreted as described in [RFC 2119](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc2119):

- **MUST** — an absolute requirement of the standard
- **MUST NOT** — an absolute prohibition
- **SHOULD** — recommended; valid reasons to deviate may exist but must be weighed carefully
- **SHOULD NOT** — not recommended; valid reasons may exist but must be weighed carefully
- **MAY** — optional; the standard neither requires nor discourages it

Lowercase uses of these words carry their ordinary meaning and are not normative.

## Scope

**This standard covers:**
- The organizational topology of a local business digital ecosystem (the LEBOSS Reference Model)
- The five Foundation Principles that govern compliant system design
- The data ownership doctrine: what primary operational data is, who owns it, and what access to it requires
- The responsibilities of service providers operating within a LEBOSS-aligned system
- Interim conformance criteria for LEBOSS-aligned systems

**This standard explicitly defers to future versions:**
- Formal conformance certification mechanisms
- Technical API or interface specifications for compliant implementations
- Ownership transfer and succession protocols
- Satellite-specific compliance rules
- Normative requirements for AI and machine learning use of primary operational data
- Reference architecture diagrams

These deferred items are captured in the gap register at [proposals/0.0.2/proposal.md](../proposals/0.0.2/proposal.md).

## 1. Introduction

LEBOSS — **Local Entrepreneur Business Operating System Standards** — is a framework for organizing, securing, and governing the digital operations of local businesses.

It is also a declaration.

At its core, LEBOSS asserts a simple proposition: the data generated by a local business belongs to the owner of that business. Not to the software provider. Not to the cloud platform. Not to the marketing agency that built the website. To the owner.

This standard defines the architectural vocabulary — and the normative obligations — that make that proposition technically enforceable, not merely aspirational.

LEBOSS is not a product. It is not a platform. It is a standard — a shared language and set of rules that developers, service providers, and business owners can use to build systems in which data sovereignty is structurally guaranteed rather than contractually promised.

---

## 2. The Problem We Are Naming

Consider what happens when a local business — a plumbing company, a dental practice, a restaurant, a landscaping service — adopts modern software tools.

They sign up for a scheduling platform. A CRM. A payment processor. A marketing automation suite. A Google Business profile. A Yelp listing. A Facebook page.

Within months, that business's operational data — its customers, its appointments, its revenue history, its reviews, its communications — lives primarily inside systems owned and controlled by corporations who are not that business. The business owner holds a login. They do not hold their data.

When the contract ends, the data often stays behind, or leaves in formats that are deliberately difficult to use elsewhere. When the platform changes its pricing, the business has little leverage. When the platform shuts down a feature, the business adapts or loses it. When the platform sells, the business has no say.

This is not an accident. It is a design.

The current industry model concentrates data ownership inside provider infrastructure as a mechanism of market control. Switching costs are high because migration costs are high because data portability is low — by design.

LEBOSS is a response to this design. It proposes an alternative architecture in which:

- The business owner's data lives in infrastructure the business controls or explicitly authorizes
- Service providers are granted access to that data, not possession of it
- Every integration is a negotiated relationship, not a data extraction

## 3. Philosophical Foundation

### 3.1 Data Is Property

The operational data of a local business — its customer records, transaction history, communications, operational metrics, reviews, and derived analytics — is the property of the business that generated it.

This is not merely an ethical claim. It is the logical extension of property rights already recognized in physical commerce. A plumber's customer list, kept in a paper ledger, belongs to the plumber. Moving that list into software does not transfer ownership. The fact that the data lives on a vendor's server does not make it the vendor's data.

LEBOSS holds that ownership follows the source of the data, not the location of its storage.

### 3.2 Infrastructure Location Determines Ownership Power

Ownership in practice is not only about legal right. It is about operational control. A business owner may technically own their data while a platform holds it — but if that platform can delete it, restrict access to it, or use it for purposes beyond the original agreement, then ownership is nominal.

LEBOSS recognizes that where data is stored, processed, and accessible determines the real distribution of ownership power. The standard therefore addresses infrastructure topology as a first-class concern — not merely a technical detail.

### 3.3 Access Must Be Explicitly Granted

Under LEBOSS, service providers do not inherit access to business data by virtue of being paid for a service. Access is a discrete act, explicitly granted by the business owner, scoped to what is necessary for the service, and revocable.

This mirrors the principle of least privilege in information security — extended to apply to the entire vendor relationship, not just internal systems.

### 3.4 Service Providers Are Stewards, Not Owners

A service provider who manages software on behalf of a local business is a steward of that business's data. They act on the owner's behalf. They hold data in trust. They are accountable to the owner for how that data is handled, transformed, retained, and disposed of.

A LEBOSS-compliant service provider cannot use a client's data — beyond what was explicitly authorized — for their own benefit.

### 3.5 Ownership Must Be Technically Enforceable

Good intentions are insufficient. A vendor who agrees in contract to treat business data responsibly but controls all infrastructure can violate that agreement with little consequence. LEBOSS insists that data ownership must be architecturally expressed — built into the topology of systems — so that it is enforceable without depending on the goodwill of any particular vendor.

## 4. Core Foundation Principles

LEBOSS is built on five principles that together define how a compliant system **MUST** behave. These principles govern both the organizational structure of business data and the obligations of those who build and maintain systems that handle it.

### Principle 1: Clarity

Every part of a business — its people, brands, workflows, and data — **MUST** have a clearly defined role and place within the system.

Implementations **MUST NOT** leave ownership, accountability, or access scope ambiguous. Ambiguity about who owns a record, who authorized an integration, or which entity a dataset belongs to is the condition under which data appropriation occurs.

### Principle 2: Modularity

Business operations and capabilities **MUST** be organized so that individual components can be adopted, replaced, or removed without requiring reconstruction of the broader system.

Implementations **MUST NOT** entangle components in a way that makes any single provider or capability irreplaceable. Modularity is the structural guarantee of freedom of choice.

### Principle 3: Security

Data **MUST** be separated by business entity. Access **MUST** follow the principle of least privilege. All data operations **MUST** be auditable.

Security in LEBOSS is a structural property of the architecture. No party **MAY** access more data than they have been explicitly authorized to access.

### Principle 4: Legacy and Continuity

Systems built to LEBOSS standards **MUST** be designed for long-term resilience, transparent ownership transitions, and deliberate succession planning.

Data **MUST** remain accessible and portable through ownership transitions. Implementations **MUST NOT** bind data to a specific person, role, or service agreement in a way that makes it inaccessible upon that person's departure or that agreement's termination.

### Principle 5: Extensibility

LEBOSS-compliant systems **MUST** allow new capabilities, workflows, and integrations to be added without disrupting existing operations.

No capability **SHOULD** be entangled with others in a way that makes its removal or replacement destructive to the system as a whole.

## 5. The LEBOSS Reference Model

LEBOSS describes a business ecosystem using six hierarchical elements, named using a spatial metaphor. This naming is **illustrative**, not prescriptive.

### 5.1 Naming Disclaimer

Implementations **MAY** use any terminology to describe the structures defined in this section. Implementations **MUST NOT** be required to adopt the spatial names (Universe, Galaxy, Star, Planet, Moon, Satellite) in order to claim LEBOSS alignment.

Any system that preserves the underlying ownership hierarchy, data boundaries, dependency rules, and access control relationships defined in this section **MAY** be described as LEBOSS-aligned regardless of the internal names used for those structures.

The spatial names are used throughout this document as a shared vocabulary for describing the reference model. They are a communication aid, not a compliance requirement.

### 5.2 Element Definitions

#### Element 0: Universe

**The foundational business entity.**

The Universe is the root owner. It represents the person, family, or registered legal entity that owns the business enterprise. Everything else in the LEBOSS hierarchy is owned by, or exists in relationship to, the Universe.

**Role:** Owns all other elements — brands, data, assets, permissions, and integrations.

**Characteristics:**
- Controls system-wide settings, legal information, and succession parameters
- Manages ownership transitions and high-level governance of the entire ecosystem
- MAY encompass multiple brands, DBAs, or distinct legal ventures
- Is the ultimate authority over all data access decisions

**Example:** Smith & Sons LLC; The Patel Family Enterprises

The Universe is not a software construct. It is a legal and organizational reality that LEBOSS-compliant systems **MUST** reflect.

---

#### Element 1: Galaxy

**An individual brand, business line, or operating company owned by the Universe.**

The Galaxy represents a distinct venture within the broader enterprise. A single Universe **MAY** contain multiple Galaxies — each with its own identity, operations, and data environment.

**Role:** Organizes and separates distinct business lines, brands, or entities.

**Characteristics:**
- Maintains its own branding, asset libraries, team structures, and operational settings
- Provides clear legal, financial, and operational separation between entities
- Shares the ownership hierarchy of its Universe while maintaining its own data boundary

**Example:** Smith Plumbing, Smith Electrical, and Smith HVAC as three Galaxies under the Smith & Sons Universe

---

#### Element 2: Star

**The customer experience — the interfaces through which the outside world interacts with the business.**

The Star is what customers see: websites, mobile applications, booking portals, kiosks, and any other interface through which customers, partners, or stakeholders make contact.

**Role:** Delivers value and manages relationships at the customer boundary.

**Characteristics:**
- Each Star is tailored to its audience (public-facing website vs. internal staff portal)
- MAY support distinct branding, languages, and workflows per Galaxy or Universe
- Defines the scope of what external parties can see and do

**Example:** smithplumbing.com; a customer booking app; an internal dispatch portal

Without a Planet to support it, a Star cannot function. Without a Star, a Planet serves no one.

---

#### Element 3: Planet

**The backend service that powers a Star.**

The Planet is the operational engine behind the customer experience. It provides the functionality — scheduling, order management, inventory, authentication, data persistence — that makes a Star meaningful.

**Role:** Powers the Star by providing backend services and business logic.

**Characteristics:**
- Each Planet serves one or more Stars
- MAY be shared across multiple Stars or Galaxies where appropriate
- MUST be isolated so that updates or replacements do not break customer-facing interfaces
- MAY be operated by the business itself or managed by a compliant service provider
- **MUST** maintain data separation from other Planets in the same or different Galaxies

**Example:** A booking API; a product inventory service; a customer authentication backend

---

#### Element 4: Moon

**A natural satellite — company-owned internal capabilities that orbit a Planet or Star.**

The Moon represents modular business capabilities owned and operated by the business. These are internal resources — APIs, workflow engines, analytics modules, billing systems — that provide specialized functionality within the LEBOSS ecosystem.

**Role:** Adds functional depth to the ecosystem through company-owned modular capabilities.

**Characteristics:**
- Moons are company-owned, company-operated resources
- MAY be enabled, configured, or upgraded independently without affecting other elements
- Access MAY be restricted by role, team, or business unit
- MAY connect to Planets to provide or consume data and workflows
- Distinct from Satellites in that the business retains full operational control

> **Implementation Note:** Common functional categories for Moon-equivalent components include sales and lead management, service delivery and fulfillment, and accounting and billing. Implementations are not required to name or organize these modules in any particular way.

---

#### Element 5: Satellite

**An artificial satellite — third-party integrations and external service connections.**

The Satellite represents integrations with platforms and services operated outside the business. These are managed resources beyond the business's direct control — external platforms that the business connects to in order to extend its reach, automate workflows, or synchronize data.

**Role:** Connects the business ecosystem to external platforms and data sources.

**Characteristics:**
- Satellites are managed resources outside the company's direct operational control
- Each Satellite **SHOULD** be scoped to one external platform or data source
- MAY be enabled or disabled independently for compliance or strategic reasons
- **MUST NOT** become a path through which primary operational data leaves the business's control without explicit authorization
- All data flows in or out through Satellites **MUST** be logged and auditable

**Example:** A Google Business Profile integration; a Facebook Ads connection; a QuickBooks sync; a Stripe payments connection

Satellites are the point of greatest data sovereignty risk. A LEBOSS-compliant system **MUST** treat every Satellite as a boundary requiring explicit authorization and ongoing audit.

---

### 5.3 Element Hierarchy Summary

| Element | Name | Role | Data Boundary |
|---------|------|------|---------------|
| 0 | Universe | Root Owner | All other elements |
| 1 | Galaxy | Brand/Business Entity | Stars, Planets, Moons within it |
| 2 | Star | Customer Interface | User-facing experience |
| 3 | Planet | Backend Service | Business logic, data persistence |
| 4 | Moon | Internal Capability | Company-owned resources |
| 5 | Satellite | External Integration | Third-party connection boundary |

### 5.4 Dependency Rules

The following dependency rules govern valid LEBOSS topologies. An implementation claiming LEBOSS alignment **MUST** preserve these relationships, regardless of the names used internally.

1. A backend service component **MUST** serve at least one customer interface. A backend with no associated interface has no purpose in the hierarchy.
2. A customer interface **MUST** be supported by at least one backend service. An interface with no backend cannot deliver value.
3. An internal capability component **MUST** operate within the data boundary of a single brand entity.
4. An external integration component **MUST** be explicitly authorized at the brand entity or root owner level before it **MAY** receive any data.
5. A brand entity **MUST** belong to exactly one root owner.
6. A customer interface **MUST** belong to exactly one brand entity.

## 6. Data Ownership Doctrine

The following rules describe the data ownership requirements that LEBOSS-compliant systems **MUST** implement.

### 6.1 Primary Data Ownership

All primary operational data generated within a LEBOSS-compliant system is owned by the root owner (Universe) to which it belongs.

Primary operational data includes but is not limited to:
- Customer records and contact information
- Transaction and payment history
- Appointment and scheduling records
- Communications between the business and its customers
- Inventory and asset records
- Employee and team records

### 6.2 Derived Data

Service providers who operate infrastructure on behalf of a LEBOSS-compliant business **MAY** collect limited derived data — specifically, operational telemetry necessary to provide and improve the service — subject to the following conditions:

- The type of derived data collected **MUST** be disclosed in advance and consented to
- Derived data **MUST NOT** include primary operational data without explicit authorization
- Derived data **MUST NOT** be used to benefit third parties without explicit authorization
- Derived data **MUST** be deleted upon termination of the service relationship, unless the business consents otherwise

### 6.3 Access Grants

No service provider **MAY** access primary operational data without an explicit, scoped access grant issued by the Universe owner or their designated delegate.

Access grants **MUST** specify:
- Which elements or equivalent structures the grant covers
- Which operations are permitted (read, write, transform, export)
- The duration or conditions under which the grant expires
- The purpose for which access is granted

Access grants are revocable at any time by the Universe owner.

### 6.4 Data Portability

A LEBOSS-compliant system **MUST** provide the Universe owner with the ability to export all primary operational data in a standard, machine-readable format at any time.

Data portability is a requirement of ownership. A system that cannot return data to the owner is a system that has appropriated it.

### 6.5 Data Residency

The Universe owner **MUST** be informed of where primary operational data is stored and processed, including the jurisdiction of that infrastructure.

The Universe owner retains the right to require that data reside within specific jurisdictions where such requirements are legally enforceable or operationally necessary.

---

## 7. Service Provider Responsibilities

A service provider operating within a LEBOSS-compliant ecosystem — whether building a customer interface, managing a backend service, operating an internal capability, or connecting an external integration — accepts the following responsibilities.

### 7.1 Act as Steward, Not Owner

The service provider acknowledges that primary operational data belongs to the business owner and that the provider acts as a steward of that data. The provider does not acquire ownership of data through access to it.

### 7.2 Operate Within Granted Access

The service provider **MUST** operate only within the scope of explicitly granted access. Any access to data beyond the scope of the grant is a violation of this standard.

### 7.3 Maintain Audit Records

The service provider **MUST** maintain complete audit records of all data access and transformations performed under the grant. These records **MUST** be available to the Universe owner upon request.

### 7.4 Ensure Portability Upon Exit

Upon termination of a service relationship, the provider **MUST** facilitate the complete and timely export of all primary operational data in a machine-readable format. The provider **MUST NOT** withhold, delay, or degrade the quality of this export.

### 7.5 Disclose Subprocessors

If the service provider uses third-party services (including cloud infrastructure, analytics platforms, or subcontractors) that have access to primary operational data, these subprocessors **MUST** be disclosed to the Universe owner and are subject to the same requirements under this standard.

### 7.6 No Secondary Use

The service provider **MUST NOT** use primary operational data for any purpose beyond delivering the contracted service — including training machine learning models, building aggregate datasets for sale, or improving services provided to other customers — without the explicit written consent of the Universe owner.

## 8. Conformance

A system may be described as **LEBOSS-aligned** if it satisfies all of the following criteria:

### 8.1 Structural Alignment

1. The system organizes its architecture to reflect the ownership hierarchy and data boundaries defined in Section 5, regardless of the internal names used.
2. Each data environment is associated with exactly one governing entity (root owner equivalent).
3. Brand-level data boundaries are maintained — data belonging to one brand entity **MUST NOT** be accessible to another brand entity without an explicit cross-entity access grant.

### 8.2 Data Ownership Alignment

4. Primary operational data is owned by the governing entity, not by any service provider.
5. Service providers operate under explicit, scoped access grants as defined in Section 6.3.
6. The governing entity can export all primary operational data at any time in a machine-readable format.
7. The governing entity is informed of where primary operational data is stored and processed.

### 8.3 Service Provider Alignment

8. All service providers maintain audit records of data access and transformations.
9. Upon exit, all primary operational data is returned in a portable, machine-readable format.
10. Subprocessors with access to primary operational data are disclosed.
11. Primary operational data is not used for secondary purposes without explicit consent.

### 8.4 Security Alignment

12. Data is separated by business entity at the architectural level.
13. Access follows least privilege — no party accesses more data than their explicit grant authorizes.
14. All external integration boundaries are explicitly authorized and audited.

### 8.5 Non-Conformance

A system **MUST NOT** be described as LEBOSS-aligned if it:
- Allows service providers to retain primary operational data after termination
- Does not support data export by the governing entity
- Allows external integrations to receive data without explicit authorization
- Lacks auditable records of data access

Formal conformance certification criteria will be defined in a subsequent version of the standard.

## 9. Relationship to Other Standards

LEBOSS is not a replacement for existing security or data standards. It operates at the organizational and architectural layer.

LEBOSS-compliant systems **SHOULD** be built in a manner consistent with:

- Applicable data protection regulations (GDPR, CCPA, and equivalents)
- The principle of least privilege as defined in NIST SP 800-53
- Open data portability initiatives including the Data Transfer Project

Where conflicts exist between LEBOSS requirements and applicable law, applicable law governs.

## 11. Governance Objects

The normative rules in this standard and in the rule register (`leboss-normative-rules-0.0.2.md`) require that access be explicitly granted, that integrations be explicitly authorized, and that all governed events be auditable. These requirements can only be satisfied if the structures that record those facts are defined.

Governance objects are the structured data primitives that give operational form to these rules. They define what must exist — the required fields and normative properties of grants, authorizations, and event records — without prescribing implementation format or protocol.

Three governance objects are defined in LEBOSS 0.0.3:

| Object | Purpose | Rules Addressed |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **Access Grant** | Records explicit authorization for a party to access primary operational data | LEBOSS-ACC-1, ACC-2, ACC-3, ACC-4 |
| **Integration Descriptor** | Records authorization of an external integration to receive data | LEBOSS-ACC-5, ARCH-10, ARCH-11 |
| **Audit Record** | Records governed events for auditability and compliance | LEBOSS-SEC-3, SVC-3, SVC-4 |

Full object definitions, including required fields, optional fields, and object-level normative requirements, are defined in:

- [`standards/objects/access-grant.md`](objects/access-grant.md)
- [`standards/objects/integration-descriptor.md`](objects/integration-descriptor.md)
- [`standards/objects/audit-record.md`](objects/audit-record.md)

The governance object model, including lifecycle concepts and format neutrality, is defined in [`standards/leboss-governance-objects.md`](leboss-governance-objects.md).

Implementations claiming LEBOSS alignment **MUST** maintain governance objects that satisfy the normative requirements defined in the individual object specifications.

## 12. Access Grant Protocol

Governance objects define structure. Protocols define behavior.

The Access Grant Protocol specifies the lifecycle and interaction rules that make Access Grants operational: how grants are issued, how they are validated before each access attempt, how revocation takes immediate effect, and how expiration is evaluated automatically.

The protocol operationalizes the following rules from this standard:

| Rule | Protocol Section |
|------|-----------------|
| LEBOSS-ACC-1 — no access without explicit grant | §5 Validation |
| LEBOSS-ACC-2 — grants must specify scope, operations, duration, purpose | §4 Issuance |
| LEBOSS-ACC-3 — grants are revocable at any time | §6 Revocation |
| LEBOSS-ACC-4 — providers must operate within granted access | §5 Validation |

**Key behavioral requirements:**

- Systems MUST validate grants at the time of each access attempt across a defined seven-step sequence: grant existence, issuer authority, subject match, expiration, revocation, scope, and operation.
- Revocation MUST take effect immediately. No caching, deferral, or queuing of revocation is permitted.
- Grant expiration is automatic — systems must evaluate expiry conditions at every access attempt.
- All grant lifecycle events (issuance, access granted, access denied, revocation, expiration) MUST generate Audit Records.

The full protocol, including 17 normative rules (LEBOSS-AGP-1 through AGP-17) and defined lifecycle state machine, is specified in [`standards/leboss-access-grant-protocol.md`](leboss-access-grant-protocol.md).

## 13. Integration Descriptor Protocol

External integrations are the point of greatest data sovereignty risk in the LEBOSS reference model. The Integration Descriptor Protocol makes integrations governed actors — with explicit lifecycle state, enforced authorization, and full audit coverage.

The protocol defines five lifecycle states: `registered`, `authorized`, `active`, `suspended`, and `deactivated`. Registration does not confer authorization. Authorization requires a valid, linked Access Grant. Active integrations must pass a five-step validation sequence before any data operation. Suspension is immediate and reversible; deactivation is immediate and permanent.

The protocol operationalizes the following rules from this standard:

| Rule | Protocol Section |
|------|-----------------|
| LEBOSS-ACC-5 — integrations must be explicitly authorized before receiving data | §4 Registration, §5 Authorization |
| LEBOSS-ARCH-10 — integrations must not become unauthorized data exit paths | §6 Operational Validation |
| LEBOSS-ARCH-11 — all data flows through integrations must be logged | §8 Audit Requirements |

**Key behavioral requirements:**

- An Integration Descriptor MUST exist before any external integration receives data. Registration is not authorization.
- Integrations MUST hold a valid, linked Access Grant to transition to `active` state.
- If an integration's linked Access Grant is revoked, the integration MUST be immediately suspended — no separate suspension command is required.
- Suspension MUST take effect immediately. Deactivation is permanent — a new descriptor and grant are required for reconnection.
- All lifecycle events MUST generate Audit Records including `integration_id` and, for data operations, `grant_id`.

The full protocol, including 26 normative rules (LEBOSS-IDP-1 through IDP-26) and defined lifecycle state machine, is specified in [`standards/leboss-integration-protocol.md`](leboss-integration-protocol.md).

## 14. Audit Record Collection Protocol

Prior protocols require specific audit records across the Access Grant and Integration Descriptor lifecycles. The Audit Record Collection Protocol defines the system behaviors that make those requirements enforceable: how events are captured, how records are correlated to the governance objects that produced them, how long they must be retained, and how their integrity is verified.

The protocol operationalizes the following rules from this standard:

| Rule | Protocol Section |
|------|-----------------|
| LEBOSS-SEC-3 — all data operations must be auditable | §3 Audit Event Capture |
| LEBOSS-SVC-3 — service providers must maintain complete audit records | §3 Audit Event Capture, §5 Retention |
| LEBOSS-SVC-4 — audit records must be available to the governing entity | §7 Audit Verification |
| LEBOSS-OBJ-AR-1 — Audit Records must be created for every governed event | §3 Audit Event Capture |
| LEBOSS-OBJ-AR-3 — Audit Records must not be modified after creation | §6 Integrity |
| LEBOSS-OBJ-AR-4 — Audit Records must be retained for a minimum of 36 months | §5 Retention |

**Key behavioral requirements:**

- A LEBOSS-compliant system MUST generate an Audit Record for every required governance event. Absence of a required record is a compliance violation.
- Audit Records MUST be created at the time of the event — retroactive creation, batching, and deferred writing are not permitted.
- Where a data operation is authorized by an Access Grant, the Audit Record MUST include the `grant_id`. Where it involves an integration, it MUST include the `integration_id`.
- Cascade events (such as grant revocation triggering integration suspension) MUST each produce their own Audit Record, with records linked by shared identifier references.
- Audit Records MUST NOT be deleted before the minimum 36-month retention period expires. Deletion controls MUST be enforced at the storage layer.
- Systems MUST store Audit Records in a manner that makes modification after creation detectable. Any tampering attempt MUST itself be recorded.
- The governing entity MUST be able to query and verify audit records without requiring cooperation from the party under review.

The full protocol, including 24 normative rules (LEBOSS-ACP-1 through ACP-24) and defined capture, correlation, retention, and integrity requirements, is specified in [`standards/leboss-audit-protocol.md`](leboss-audit-protocol.md).
